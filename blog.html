Zero Trust Architecture: The Key to Securing Government Networks in 2025

Posted on May 16, 2025 by Watchdog Cybersecurity Team

As cyber threats evolve, government agencies face increasing pressure to protect sensitive data. Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) has emerged as a cornerstone of modern cybersecurity, mandated by Executive Order 14028 and NIST SP 800-207. In this post, we explore how ZTA mitigates risks in enterprise systems like Azure and Red Hat Enterprise Linux, drawing on recent vulnerabilities and best practices.

The traditional "trust but verify" model is obsolete in today’s threat landscape, where nation-state actors and ransomware groups exploit even minor vulnerabilities. Zero Trust assumes no user or device is inherently trustworthy, requiring continuous verification. For government networks, this is critical—NIST’s 2024 updates to SP 800-207 emphasize ZTA’s role in securing cloud-based systems like Azure, which 60% of federal agencies now use (NIST). Similarly, Red Hat Enterprise Linux, widely deployed in DoD environments, benefits from ZTA’s granular access controls.

Recent CVEs highlight the need for ZTA. For example, CVE-2024-8190, an Ivanti CSA command injection flaw, allowed remote code execution on Azure-integrated Windows servers. A ZTA approach, with strict identity verification and micro-segmentation, could have limited the attack surface. Likewise, CVE-2024-6409 in OpenSSH on RHEL 9 exposed systems to remote exploits, underscoring the need for least-privilege access.

Implementing ZTA involves three pillars: identity, device, and data security. For Azure, this means leveraging Azure AD Conditional Access and MFA. For RHEL, Red Hat Identity Management (IdM) integrates with ZTA frameworks to enforce role-based access.
